Where I Began

Ever since 6th grade, I've loved to mess around with technology. I used to love pushing the boundaries and modding my Xbox 360, my ipod, and making changes that nobody else had access to. I would find glitches in video games and install other creators' mods to 1-up my friends.

When I learned to code with Python my freshman year of college, I felt like it was too much to learn, and I was overwhelmed by all of the information. But as I went through my degree, I stuck with it, and it definitely paid off.

Second Year of College

After scraping the surface of coding with Python, I learned Java. That same year, I learned about how operating sytems work, Unix command prompt, computer organization, and computer graphics with WebGL. I also learned some other subjects like calculus 2, discrete structures, and physics.

To top it off, I was working for IT at my school as a support technician and learning about computer software, hardware repairs, OS imaging, and creating batch scripts.

Junior Year

This was a big year for me, as I finally started as an assistant web dev. Now that I was working for IT as a technician and as a developer, I was bettering my skills with HTML, Bootstrap, JQuery, C#, ASP.NET Core, SQL Server Management Studio, and LINQ queries.

In class, I was being taught about android apps/programming, CPU architecture, and parallel computing. It was also during this year that I took a database programming class and created my senior capstone -- a compiler in the C language.

Being a Senior

During the final year of college, I was able to relax a bit, but I spent most of my time looking for jobs in the web development field. I searched for a good amount of time, but I wasn't having much luck, so I focused on bettering my skills at my jobs on campus.

I also was focused on my relationships with my brothers/best-friends in my Fraternity as the Vice President. While working on my classwork for courses like differential equations, networking, and my second senior capstone in software engineering, I took an even deeper interest in my future as an aspiring web developer.

GrapeCity

After graduating during the 2020 pandemic, I continued working for IT at my university for a few months until I randomly took a job as an estimator at a local cabinet business. I didn't feel fulfilled, I hated my job, and I quickly fell into a depression after a breakup with my girlfriend at the time.

After a few months of continuing job apps, the light shined through and I got a call for an interview with a company named GrapeCity. They wanted me to be a "Technical Engagement Engineer," which basically meant I would help product customers with their web development coding issues. It was here where I got serious about learning .NET, bettering my HTML and C# skills, as well as learning Vanilla JS and CSS without any frameworks.

Life since 2021

Since getting hired as an ActiveReports.NET Technical Engagement Engineer for GrapeCity, I've since taken multiple long-haul courses on Udemy bettering my skills in HTML, CSS, JavaScript, React, and C#. Because our customers use a variety of different tech stacks in their code, I was able to gain experience with lots of different apps, technologies, and tools -- I've worked with ASP.NET 5 and 6, Winforms, WPF apps using C# and VB.NET.

Around March 2022, I had the opportunity to transfer to the ActiveReportsJS product within that same company -- basically the same product, but aimed at front-end applications that use remote JSON data for their reporting needs. With this, I was able to learn about React and Angular, write technical pieces on a few frameworks and prevelant coding questions, and get more familiar with REST and OData endpoints.

My Dev Experience

How was this website made?

This page was made with HTML5, CSS3, and Vanilla JS on the front end. I wanted to make something that looks beautiful without the use of many front-end frameworks or libaries. In the backend, the site is using Mongoose to hook up a MongoDB collection. I am using Express.js to route to various REST API endpoints.


If you add /users to the base URL of this site, you can get a full list of users that have been created in my DB. If you want to test out some parameters, you can go navigate to /user/:id to see if a user exists with a certain id.

How does this work?

Simply create, update, or delete a user's record by with an id number. After sucessfully submitting one of the forms, the page should refresh. You can make sure that the data was added to the site's collection by adding /user/:id (where :id is the actual number) to the end of this page's URL.
